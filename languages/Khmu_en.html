1. About Khmu

The Khmu (other spellings: Khamu, Khammu, Khamou or Kemu) mainly live in northern Laos: Nam-Tha, Phôngsali, Sam-Neua and Luang Phrabang; they are considered autochthonous to their current living area. There are some groups located in southwestern China (Yunnan Province), Vietnam (Son-Tay and Cua-Rao), Burma and Thailand (Chiang-Ray and Nan Province).

They speak Khmu, a language which is a member of the Khmuic branch of the Mon-Khmer family (Austroasiatic). Khmu is closely related to languages such as Ksingmul (Xinh Mun) in Vietnam and Laos, and Phong (Kniang) in Laos (see Ferlus 1977; Sidwell 2014). Khmu is a wonderful introduction to typical Mon-Khmer features such as infixal morphology (Ferlus 1977a) and sesquisyllabic structure (Matisoff 1973).

There are several Khmu dialects which present some differences in their consonant inventories and in their prosodic systems (Suwilai 2001, 2004). On the basis of these differences, Khmu can be subdivided into two main dialects: Western Khmu has fewer consonant phonemes and presents a two-way contrast in register (lax/breathy vs. tense/modal), further evolving towards a tonal contrast, whereas Eastern Khmu has neither register distinction nor tone distinction. Eastern Khmu preserves voicing oppositions among initial stops (voiced, unvoiced, and aspirated series) and initial sonorants (voiced, unvoiced, and glottalized series) (Suwilai et al. 2002). These two dialects have been the object of in-depth investigations (see Svantesson 1983; Suwilai 2004; Lindell et al. 1979, 1981).

Khmu remains a vigorous language and there remains considerable room for research. 


2. The archived documents

Michel Ferlus collected Khmu data from 1963 to the late 70's. In the course of his field trips, he became a fluent speaker of this language. Ferlus's Khmu data represents his most extensive collection: he collected enough material to produce a dictionary and a grammar, and to edit a rich collection of fully transcribed and annoted texts. These constitute valuable resources to explore the language as well as the customs and oral tradition of the Khmu people. Their contents are also of interest to historians, anthropologists, and sociologists.  

These recordings mainly comprise narratives and songs. To take only one example illustrating their richness, the Chuang/Cheuang story is of key importance to several cultures of the area. (About this epic, see Ferlus, Michel. 1979. "Le récit khamou de Chuang et ses implications historiques pour le Nord-Laos", in Asie du sud-est et Monde Insulindien, vol. 10, no. 2-4.) In his discussion of the Tai version of this narrative, Chamberlain (1990) explains that "the epic of Thao Hung or Cheuang is perhaps the most important piece of literature from the Southeast Asian mainland, as monumental to the study of Southeast Asia civilization as the Ramayana is to India or the Iliad is to the West. [...] For students of history, the work represents a detailed account of a place and period in Southeast Asian history that is a virtual vacuum in our knowledge, but a place and period that was undoubedly the site of great upheavals as the pre-Buddhist Tais migrated westward from northern Vietnam through northern Laos, to northern Tailand, Burma, Yunnan, and Assam, conquering the older Austroasiatic populations as they went. [...] In essence, Cheuang is the most comprehensive account avaible of ancient Tai civilization, and as such it is wealth of information [...] for the entire Tai-speaking world [...]." (Chamberlain, James. 1990. "Thao Hung or Cheuang: A Tai Epic Poem" in Mon-Khmer Studies, vol. 18-19, pp. 14-34.)

The resources made available here are intended for a broad audience of linguists interested by the morphological and phonological aspects of Mon-Khmer languages. At present, Michel Ferlus's typed transcriptions of texts and lexicographic materials have not yet been digitized; persons interested to contribute in the task are welcome to get in touch. 

A technical note: in cases where the latitude and longitude of the place of recording could not be determined with precision, the values indicated, by default, are those of the Luang Prabang area (lat. 20, long. 102; these cases can be identified automatically in the database, as only an integer figure is provided). We would be most grateful if visitors of this site could help us determine the present-day coordinates of these locations.

Ferlus worked with several collaborators, who acted as respondent during the collection of tales to help rekindle the storyteller's eloquence and asked questions during interviews. Not all participants in the recordings have been identified so far; we hope to be able to add more and more pieces of this type of information to the metadata as these legacy data continue to be processed.
