1. About Khmu

The Khmu (other spellings: Khamu, Khammu, Khamou or Kemu) mainly live in northern Laos: Nam-Tha, Phôngsali, Sam-Neua and Luang Phrabang; they are considered autochthonous to their current living area. There are some groups located in southwestern China (Yunnan Province), Vietnam (Son-Tay and Cua-Rao), Burma and Thailand (Chiang-Ray and Nan Province).

They speak Khmu, a language which is a member of the Khmuic branch of the Austroasiatic family. This language is closely related to the Mon-Khmer group such as Ksingmul (Xinh Mun) in Vietnam and Laos or Phong (Kniang) in Laos (see Ferlus 1977; Sidwell 2014). Khmu is a worderful introduction to the typical Mon-Khmer features such as infixal morphology (Ferlus 1977a) and sesquisyllabic structures (Matisoff 1973).

There are several Khmu dialects which present some differences in their consonant inventories and in the existence of  pitch registers (Suwilai 2001, 2004). According these differences, Khmu can be subdivided in two main dialects: Western Khmu has fewer consonant phonemes and presents a two-way contrast in register (lax/breathy vs. tense/modal) whereas Eastern Khmu has not either register distinction or tone distinction. Instead of these kinds of distinction, Eastern Khmu uses a distinction of stops (voiced, unvoiced, aspirated) and a distinction of nasals (voiced, unvoiced, glottalized) in the initial syllable position (Suwilai et al. 2002). Different studies deal with these two dialects (see Svantesson 1983; Suwilai 2004; Lindell et al. 1979, 1981).

Khmu remains a still vigorous language and has yet to be fully exploited. The resources available here are intented to a large public, interested by both morphological and phonological aspects of Mon-Khmer languages of which Khmu is one representative. The present data may constitute a good base to begin or to complete a research about Khmu language, and more generally about Mon-Khmer languages.


2. The archived documents

Michel Ferlus collected Khmu data from 1963 to the late 70's. In the course of his different fieldworks, he became a fluent speaker of this language. Ferlus' Khmu data represents his most extensive fieldwork: he collected enough material to produce a dictionary and a grammar, and to edit a rich collection of fully transcribed and annoted texts about clan stories and lengendaries, which constitute valuable resources to explain both historical linguistic traditions and actual habits and customs of Khmu people.

These recordings mainly include narratives and songs. In order to have an idea of the wealth of Ferlus' Khmu collection, we can quote only one of the best known examples: the Chuang/Cheuang story. About this epic, see [i] Ferlus, Michel. 1979. "Le récit khamou de Chuang et ses implications historiques pour le Nord-Laos", in Asie du sud-est et Monde Insulindien, vol. 10, no. 2-4; [ii] Chamberlain, James. 1990. "Thao Hung or Cheuang: A Tai Epic Poem" in Mon-Khmer Studies, vol. 18-19, pp. 14-34. Here are excerpts from Chamberlain [1990]: "Today, due in part no doubt to its highly difficult vocabulary and style, the text remains obscure and its value unrecognized. Yet, the epic of Thao Hung or Cheuang is perhaps the most important piece of literature from the Southeast Asian mainland, as monumental to the study of Southeast Asia civilization as the Ramayana is to India or the Iliad is to the West. [...] For students of history, the work represents a detailed account of a place and period in Southeast Asian history that is a virtual vacuum in our knowledge, but a place and period that was undoubedly the site of great upheavals as the pre-Buddhist Tais migrated westward from northern Vietnam through northern Laos, to northern Tailand, Burma, Yunnan, and Assam, conquering the older Austroasiatic populations as they went. To students of comparative Tai linguistics, Cheuang is outstanding for its sparse use of Indic vocabulary, representing a source of unusually diverse pre-Buddhist Tai lexical items as well as providing an excellent example of poetic rhymes indicating composition prio to the devoicing sound shift that spread over East and Southeast Asia. [...] In essence, Cheuang is the most comprehensive account avaible of ancient Tai civilization, and as such it is wealth of information [...] for the entire Tai-speaking world [...]."

Ferlus worked with several collaborators, who acted as respondent during the collection of tales to help rekindle the storyteller's eloquence and asked questions during interviews.
